Why Most Patients Have Never Heard of This
Low-dose radiation therapy for osteoarthritis falls between two specialties — rheumatology and radiation oncology rarely communicate about this option, leaving patients without the full picture.

A Specialty Gap

Primary care doctors, orthopedists, and rheumatologists often manage joint pain. Radiation oncologists deliver radiation. These groups do not always connect around this treatment, so it may not be mentioned to patients.

Decades of Evidence

Low-dose radiation therapy for joint inflammation has been used in Europe for decades. The evidence exists — it simply hasn't reached most US patients or their primary care doctors.

Now Growing in the US

Major US hospitals — including Penn Medicine and Cleveland Clinic — have begun offering LDRT for osteoarthritis. The treatment is available, but awareness lags far behind.

Is This Treatment Right for You?

You may be a candidate for low-dose radiation therapy if any of the following apply to you:

  • You have been diagnosed with osteoarthritis or degenerative joint disease
  • Medications, injections, or physical therapy have not fully helped
  • You are not yet ready for — or cannot undergo — joint replacement surgery
  • You have pain in your knees, hips, hands, ankles, elbows, or shoulders
  • You are looking for a non-surgical outpatient option

About Radiant Joint Alliance

A student-led patient education initiative dedicated to closing the arthritis treatment awareness gap

Our Mission

Radiant Joint Alliance is dedicated to ensuring arthritis patients and their caregivers know about all available treatment options — including low-dose radiation therapy — so they can have informed conversations with their care team and make decisions that are right for them.

Why We Started This

Low-dose radiation therapy for osteoarthritis and degenerative joint pain is available at hospitals across the United States. Major academic medical centers — including Penn Medicine and Cleveland Clinic — have begun offering it. The treatment has decades of use in Europe.

Yet many osteoarthritis patients — including older adults who may be looking for non-surgical options — have never heard of it. Their primary care doctors often have not either.

Radiant Joint Alliance exists to bridge that gap — one patient, one community, one conversation at a time.

What We Do
  • Educate patients at senior centers, assisted living facilities, and community health fairs
  • Distribute physician-reviewed patient education materials
  • Connect patients with hospitals currently offering LDRT
  • Raise awareness among primary care physicians and caregivers

Arthritis Basics

General arthritis information, common treatment options, and how osteoarthritis differs from autoimmune arthritis

ℹ

Educational note: This page gives general information only. It is not a diagnosis or treatment recommendation. Patients should speak with their primary care doctor, rheumatologist, orthopedist, or radiation oncologist about their own situation.

What Is Arthritis?

Arthritis is a broad word for joint pain, stiffness, swelling, or inflammation. It is not one single disease. Different types of arthritis have different causes and often need different treatments.

Osteoarthritis

The most common type of arthritis. It is often related to cartilage wear, joint stress, age, prior injury, or overuse. Symptoms often include pain with activity, stiffness, and reduced mobility.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

An autoimmune inflammatory disease where the immune system attacks the joints. It usually needs rheumatology care and disease-modifying medications.

Psoriatic Arthritis

An autoimmune inflammatory arthritis linked with psoriasis. It can affect joints, tendons, spine, skin, and nails and should be managed with a rheumatology team.

Gout and Other Types

Gout is caused by uric acid crystal buildup and can cause sudden painful flares. Other causes of joint pain include injury, infection, tendon problems, and bursitis.

Common Osteoarthritis Treatment Options

Most patients start with conservative, non-surgical care. Treatment is usually layered step by step based on pain level, function, medical history, and imaging.

Exercise and Physical Therapy

Low-impact movement, strengthening, stretching, and physical therapy can reduce stiffness, improve balance, and protect the joint.

Weight and Joint Load

For weight-bearing joints such as knees and hips, reducing joint load may decrease pain and improve daily function.

Topical or Oral Medicines

Doctors may recommend topical anti-inflammatory creams, acetaminophen, or NSAIDs when safe. Medication choices depend on kidney, stomach, heart, and bleeding risks.

Braces and Assistive Devices

Braces, splints, canes, shoe inserts, or activity modifications can reduce stress on painful joints.

Joint Injections

Steroid or other injections may be considered for selected patients. Benefit varies by joint, severity, and patient history.

Surgery

For severe arthritis with major pain and disability, joint replacement or other orthopedic procedures may be discussed.

Where LDRT Fits

Low-dose radiation therapy is being used by some radiation oncology programs for osteoarthritis / degenerative joint pain when standard conservative options have not provided enough relief or when patients want to avoid or delay surgery.

It does not rebuild cartilage and it is not a cure for arthritis. The goal is symptom relief: less pain, less inflammation, and better function in carefully selected patients.

!

Important distinction: LDRT on this website refers to treatment for osteoarthritis / degenerative joint pain. It is not recommended for rheumatoid or psoriatic arthritis. Patients with autoimmune arthritis should speak with their rheumatologist first.

When to Ask for Medical Care Promptly
  • Sudden severe joint swelling, redness, warmth, or fever
  • Joint pain after a fall, injury, or accident
  • Inability to bear weight, use the joint, or perform normal daily activities
  • Joint symptoms with unexplained weight loss, rash, eye inflammation, or prolonged morning stiffness

Low-Dose Radiation Therapy for Osteoarthritis

What LDRT is, how it is delivered, typical dose schedules, and who it may help

What Is Low-Dose Radiation Therapy?

Low-dose radiation therapy (LDRT) is a non-surgical, outpatient treatment that uses a very small, carefully targeted amount of radiation directed at an arthritic joint. The goal is to reduce inflammation in the joint lining, which is a primary source of arthritis pain.

The radiation dose used is far smaller than what is used to treat cancer. Treatments are typically delivered in a series of short sessions, each taking only minutes.

No hospital stay is required. Patients go home the same day.

Key Facts at a Glance

  • Outpatient — no hospital stay needed
  • Non-surgical and non-invasive
  • Completed in a few short sessions
  • Very low radiation dose — not cancer-level
  • Used safely in Europe for decades
  • Available at growing number of US hospitals
Who May Benefit

Osteoarthritis

Patients with degenerative joint disease, particularly in the knees, hips, hands, ankles, shoulders, or feet, who have not found full relief from medications, injections, or physical therapy.

Degenerative Joint Pain

Patients with chronic joint pain related to wear-and-tear arthritis may ask whether a radiation oncology consultation is appropriate.

Important Distinction

LDRT is generally discussed for osteoarthritis, not every type of arthritis. It is not recommended for rheumatoid or psoriatic arthritis, so patients with autoimmune arthritis should speak with their rheumatologist first.

How Treatment Is Delivered

Animated Treatment Overview

This simplified animation shows the basic idea of low-dose radiation therapy. The patient is positioned on the treatment couch, the painful joint is aligned to the treatment area, and the linear accelerator delivers a brief, carefully planned low-dose beam.

1. PositionThe patient is placed comfortably on the treatment couch.
2. AlignThe painful joint is matched to the planned treatment area.
3. TreatThe LINAC delivers a brief low-dose beam to the joint.
4. FinishThe patient leaves the clinic after the short outpatient visit.
Typical Treatment Schedule

LDRT programs often use a total dose of about 3.0 Gy, split into 6 short treatments of 0.5 Gy per treatment. Treatments are commonly given 2 to 3 times per week, so the full course usually takes about 2 to 3 weeks.

3.0 Gy Total dose

Exact schedules can vary by hospital, joint location, patient history, and physician preference. Some programs may evaluate a second course later if symptoms persist or return.

How Low Is the Dose?

Approximate Dose Comparison

For osteoarthritis, one common low-dose radiation schedule is about 0.5 Gy × 6 treatments = 3 Gy total. By comparison, many cancer radiation treatment courses use much higher total doses, often around 40–80 Gy, depending on the cancer type and treatment goal.

Key takeaway: LDRT for osteoarthritis is usually only a small fraction of typical cancer radiotherapy doses. Using 3 Gy as an example, it is about 5% or less of many standard cancer treatment dose ranges.

Dose ranges are approximate and vary by disease, treatment site, and clinical plan. Patients should ask the treating radiation oncologist about the exact dose, expected benefit, and risks for their own situation.
