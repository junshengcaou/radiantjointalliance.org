The American Radium Society Guideline for LDRT

The first U.S. multidisciplinary consensus guideline on low-dose radiation therapy for osteoarthritis

In 2026, the American Radium Society (ARS) published its Appropriate Use Criteria (AUC) for low-dose radiotherapy in osteoarthritis, the first national consensus guideline in the United States to address this treatment directly. Unlike a single study, this is a formal review and recommendation from a multispecialty panel, radiation oncologists, rheumatologists, orthopedic surgeons, and a patient advocate, evaluating the full body of published evidence together.

How the Guideline Was Developed

The panel used a structured framework (PICOTS: population, intervention, comparator, outcome, timing, and study design) along with Cochrane and PRISMA methodology to evaluate the evidence. Of 548 articles identified in the initial search, 44 met inclusion criteria, with 8 more added through citation searching, all studies with at least 50 patients, published between 2010 and 2025. The panel rated appropriateness using the RAND-UCLA modified Delphi consensus method, a well-established process for turning a body of mixed-quality evidence into a formal clinical recommendation.

What the Guideline Concludes
  • LDRT is appropriate for selected patients with persistent pain and functional limitation who have not achieved adequate relief from conservative treatment (exercise, physical therapy, weight management, NSAIDs, topical medications, braces, or injections).
  • It is best suited for patients who are poor surgical candidates, or who prefer to delay or avoid surgery, and who understand both the potential benefits and the current limits of the evidence.
  • The guideline does not recommend LDRT as a first-line treatment.
  • Across the reviewed studies, overall treatment response rates ranged from 60% to 90%, with improvements in pain, joint mobility, and quality of life.
Dosing, Safety, and Patient Selection

The guideline supports the standard European regimen already described on our LDRT treatment page: 0.5 Gy per fraction, six fractions, a total of 3 Gy. Some centers consider a second course months later if the initial response is incomplete or pain returns.

On safety, the panel found very few acute side effects and no meaningful short-term toxicity in modern treatment series. Because the population receiving LDRT for osteoarthritis is typically older adults, and the doses used are far below cancer-treatment levels, the panel considered the theoretical long-term cancer risk to be very low.

Patients most likely to benefit generally have mild-to-moderate osteoarthritis, chronic pain despite standard therapy, and a desire to avoid or postpone surgery. The guideline is explicit that LDRT should be part of shared decision-making between patient and physician, not offered routinely to everyone with osteoarthritis.

Why a Multidisciplinary Guideline Matters

Most of what's been published on LDRT comes from radiation oncology alone. This guideline is different because rheumatologists and orthopedic surgeons, the specialists patients are more likely to see first, were part of the same panel and reached the same conclusion. The guideline explicitly frames LDRT as a treatment that should complement, not replace, other evidence-based osteoarthritis care, and calls for continued collaboration between radiation oncology, rheumatology, orthopedics, and primary care.

For patients, the practical significance is this: LDRT is no longer just something an individual hospital or physician has chosen to offer. It now has a documented, evidence-based national recommendation behind it, developed by the specialists across the different fields a patient's care actually touches.

ℹ

For education only. This guideline supports LDRT as an appropriate option for selected patients, not a recommendation for everyone with osteoarthritis. Talk with your doctor about whether it fits your situation.
